Power Supply Units 3.1.1

- power supply unit (PSU) 
	- The power supply unit (PSU) delivers low-voltage direct current (DC) power to PC components
	- It contains a rectifier to convert alternating current (AC) from the building to DC voltage, transformers to step down to lower voltages, and regulators to ensure consistent output with filters and regulators. 

- low line (120 VAC)  and high line (230 VAC) 
- AC voltage supply varies by country and distribution circuits, so PSUs have a wide tolerance range: 100-127 VAC for low-line and 220-240 VAC for high-line.

Wattage Rating 3.1.3

- Power (Watts) = Voltage (V) × Current (I)
- The Power Supply Unit (PSU) must provide enough wattage for all components combined.
- Typical PSU Wattage:
	- Standard desktop: 400–500 W
	- Gaming PCs: 600 W or more
	- Enterprise servers/workstations: >1000 W

- Problems from an Underpowered PSU
- System Instability: random shutdowns, reboots, crashes
- Component Damage: PSU overheating → can harm itself or other parts

- Power Distribution (Rails)
- A rail is a wire carrying current at a specific voltage.
- Modern systems mainly use the +12 V rail, since it powers CPU and GPU.
	- Output Rail (VDC)	Max Load (A)	Max Output (W)
	+3.3 V			20		130
	+5 V			20		130
	+12 V			33		396
	-12 V			0.8		9.6
	+5 V (standby)		2.5		12.5

	- Note:+3.3 V and +5 V rails often share a combined power limit.
	- The +12 V rail is the most critical for modern systems.

- Energy Efficiency
	- Efficiency = how much of the wall power (AC) becomes usable DC power.
	- Example:
		- 300 W PSU @ 75% efficiency = draws 400 W, wastes 100 W as heat.
		- More heat = more cooling needed = higher energy cost.

	- 80 PLUS Efficiency Ratings
	Rating		20% Load	50% Load	100% Load	Notes
	Bronze		82%		85%		82%		Entry-level efficiency
	Silver		85%		88%		85%		Moderate efficiency
	Gold		87%		90%		87%		Common in gaming PCs
	Platinum	90%		92%		89%		High efficiency
	Titanium	90% @10%	92%		94%		90%
	
	-ENERGY STAR 80 PLUS PSUs must be ≥80% efficient at 20–100% load.

Power Supply Connectors 3.1.4

- The Power Supply Unit (PSU) sends DC voltage to all computer components.
- Common voltage levels:
	- +3.3 VDC
	- +5 VDC
	- +12 VDC
- Voltage regulators ensure each component gets the correct amount of power.

- P1 Connector = 24-pin ATX power connector
	- Supplies main power to the motherboard.
	- Earlier systems used a 20-pin version (still backward compatible with adapters).
	- Provides +3.3 V, +5 V, +12 V, and ground connections.

- PCIe (Graphics Card) Power Connectors
	- Supply extra power to GPUs (beyond what the PCIe slot gives).
	- Common types:
		- 6-pin PCIe = up to 75 W
		- 8-pin PCIe = up to 150 W
		- 16-pin (new 12VHPWR for PCIe 5.0 GPUs) = up to 600 W
	- Tip: Always match the connector type to the GPU’s requirement to avoid underpowering it.

- Drive Power Connectors
- Molex Connector
	- 4-pin connector used for older devices like:
		- IDE hard drives
		- Optical drives
		- Some case fans
	- Provides +12 V and +5 V

- SATA Power Connector
	- Slim, 15-pin connector for modern drives (HDDs, SSDs, optical drives).
	- Provides +3.3 V, +5 V, and +12 V.

- Key Notes
	- Always ensure the connector type and voltage rating match the device.
	- Never force a connector it should fit easily.
	- Incorrect connections can cause damage to components or the PSU.

20-pin to 24-pin Motherboard Adapter 3.1.5
- ATX PSU Standards
	- The ATX (Advanced Technology eXtended) power supply standard has gone through multiple revisions.
	- These updates changed the main motherboard connector design to support newer hardware with higher power needs.


- Original ATX Connector (20-pin)
	- Early ATX motherboards used a 20-pin (2×10) P1 connector.
	- Wire color coding:
		- Yellow = +12 V
		- Red = +5 V
		- Orange = +3.3 V
		- Black = Ground
	- Tip: The 20-pin connector supplied enough power for older CPUs, RAM, and expansion cards.

- Modern ATX Connector (24-pin)
	- Modern motherboards use a 24-pin (2×12) P1 connector.
	- The extra 4 pins provide additional +12 V, +5 V, and +3.3 V power lines for newer components that draw more power (like PCIe slots and extra memory).

- 20+4-pin Compatibility
	- Many modern PSUs include a 20+4-pin adapter:
		- The extra 4 pins can detach to fit older 20-pin motherboards.
		- When combined, it becomes a full 24-pin connector for newer boards.
	- Purpose: Ensures backward compatibility between old and new motherboards.

Key Notes
	- Always check the motherboard manual for the required connector type.
	- Never force a 24-pin connector into a 20-pin socket that doesn’t support it.
	- Using the proper adapter ensures stable power delivery and prevents system damage.

Modular Power Supplies 3.1.6
- Overview
	- A modular PSU has detachable power cables, allowing you to connect only the cables your system needs.
	- This contrasts with a non-modular PSU, where all cables are permanently attached.

- Benefits of Modular PSUs
	- Reduced Clutter
		- Only necessary cables are used, which keeps the interior tidy.
	- Improved Airflow
		- Fewer cables mean less obstruction for case fans and airflow.
	- Better Cooling
		- Reduced cable clutter improves overall system cooling efficiency.

- Example
	- A non-modular PSU may have 4–5 Molex/SATA connectors, but your PC may only use 2.
	- With a modular PSU, you can remove unused cables, reducing clutter and improving airflow.

- Key Notes
	- Modular PSUs are especially useful in gaming PCs, high-performance builds, and compact cases.
	- Some high-end PSUs are fully modular (all cables detachable), while others are semi-modular (essential cables like 24-pin ATX and CPU power remain attached).

Redundant Power Supplies 3.1.7
- Overview
	- Redundant PSUs are used to maintain system uptime and prevent data loss, especially in enterprise environments.
	- Systems may have two PSUs:
		- Primary PSU: powers the system normally.
		- Redundant/failover PSU: automatically takes over if the primary fails.
- Purpose & Benefits
	- Continuous Operation
		- Essential in data centers and high-availability systems.
	- Data Protection
		- Minimizes the risk of data loss during PSU failure.
	- System Reliability
		- Keeps servers running even during power failures or PSU malfunctions.

-  Server Implementation
	- PSUs connect to a backplane, which is a circuit board providing electrical connections between components.
	- Hot-swappable PSUs:
		- Faulty PSU can be replaced without shutting down the system.
		- Critical for maintaining uptime and ensuring services remain available.

- Desktop vs Server Use
	- Desktops: Rarely use redundant PSUs because continuous uptime is less critical.
	- Servers/Enterprise systems: Standard feature due to need for reliability and data integrity.

- Redundant PSUs are a key topic for enterprise hardware setups—remember their role in hot-swapping and failover scenarios.

Fan Cooling Systems 3.1.10
- Overview
	- Computer components generate heat due to electrical resistance.
	- Excessive heat can:
		- Damage components
		- Cause system instability or crashes
	- CPUs are particularly sensitive to overheating.
	- Note: Other components like RAM, GPUs, and chipsets also need cooling.

- Purpose of Fan Cooling
	- Dissipate Heat
		- Moves hot air away from components.
	- Maintain Safe Operating Temperatures
		- Keeps CPUs, GPUs, and other devices within manufacturer-specified thermal limits.
	- Prevent Malfunctions
		- Reduces risk of crashes or permanent hardware damage.

- Key Points
	- Intel and AMD improve CPU thermal efficiency, but all CPUs require active cooling.
	- Fans are a cost-effective and common cooling solution.
	- Proper airflow in the case is important:
	- Intake fans bring in cool air
	- Exhaust fans remove hot air

- For high-performance PCs or servers, additional fans or alternative cooling (like liquid cooling) may be necessary.

Heat Sinks and Thermal Paste 3.1.11

- Overview
	- Heat sinks help dissipate heat from components to prevent overheating.
	- Two types:
		- Passive Heat Sinks
		- Active Heat Sinks
- Passive Heat Sinks
	- Also called heat spreaders.
	- Used by: memory modules (RAM), some chipsets.
	- Characteristics:
		- No fan
		- Rely on increased surface area and natural airflow to cool components

Active Heat Sinks
	- Used by: CPUs, high-end GPUs, some integrated graphics chipsets.
	- Components:
		- Metal block (copper or aluminum)	
		- Fins for increased surface area
		- Fan for forced air convection
	- Attachment: mounted to the component using thermal paste or thermal pads to eliminate air gaps for efficient heat transfer.

- Thermal Paste vs Thermal Pads
	Feature			Thermal Paste			Thermal Pad
	Heat Transfer		High efficiency			Moderate
	Ease of Application	Requires careful application	Easier to apply
	Reliability		Long-lasting if applied correctly	May degrade faster
	- Thermal paste is generally preferred for CPUs due to better heat conductivity.

- CPU Heat Sink Installation
	- Attached to CPU socket on the motherboard.
	- Secured using:
		- Retaining clips
		- Push pins (release/reset with a half-turn of a screwdriver)

Fans 3.1.12
- Overview
	- Fans are used to improve airflow and dissipate heat that passive heat sinks cannot handle.
	- Found in:
		- Power supplies
		- Chassis exhaust points
		- Heat sinks (most CPU/GPU heat sinks have fans)

- Airflow and Cooling
	- Cool air is drawn from front vents; warm air is expelled through back vents.
	- Chassis designs may include:
		- Plastic shrouds or baffles to direct airflow over the CPU
		- Clips to secure airflow channels
	- Tip: Proper airflow is essential—minimize cable clutter and cover unused expansion slots with blanking plates.

- Fan Control & Monitoring
	- Fans connect to motherboard fan power ports.
	- Thermometer sensors at each fan location:
		- Set appropriate fan speeds
		- Detect fan failures

- Maintenance
	- Fans and heat sinks lose effectiveness if dust accumulates.
	- Clean regularly using:
		- Soft brush
		- Compressed air
		- PC-approved vacuum cleaner
	- Tip: Regular cleaning improves cooling efficiency and prolongs component lifespan.

Liquid Cooling Systems  3.1.13

- Overview
	- Used in high-end gaming PCs, workstations, or PCs in high ambient temperatures.
	- Pumps coolant (usually water) through the system for more effective cooling than air alone.
	- Often quieter than multiple fans.

- Open-Loop Liquid Cooling Components
	- Water Loop / Tubing and Pump
		- Circulates coolant through the system via a reservoir.
	- Water Blocks and Brackets
		- Attached to components (CPU, GPU, etc.)
		- Remove heat by convection, similar to heat sink/fan assemblies
		- Connected to the water loop
	- Radiators and Fans
		- Positioned at air vents to expel heat from the liquid coolant
	- Tip: Think of water blocks as a “liquid heat sink” transferring heat to the radiator.

- Closed-Loop (All-In-One) Systems
	- Pre-assembled for single components (CPU or GPU)
	- Easier to install and maintain than open-loop systems
	- Typically maintenance-free for standard use

- Exam Tip:
	- Open-loop = customizable, supports multiple components, requires maintenance.
	- Closed-loop (AIO) = simpler, single-component cooling, mostly maintenance-free.

Solid-State Drives 3.2.3

-  Overview
	- SSDs use flash memory technology for persistent mass storage (non-volatile).
	- Offer much faster read speeds than mechanical HDDs.
	- More durable, shock-resistant, and energy-efficient.
	- Prices have dropped significantly, making SSDs more common in modern PCs.
	- hard disk drives (HDDs)
	- solid state drive (SSD)

Noise				Silent						Audible spinning and clicking

Hard Disk Drives 3.2.4 

- Overview
	- HDDs store data on spinning metal or glass platters coated with a magnetic material.
	- Data is read and written by read/write heads that move across the platter surfaces.
	- Each platter spins on a spindle, and the heads move via an actuator arm.

	- Think of an HDD like a record player:
	- Platters = records, heads = needle, spindle = turntable motor.

- How It Works
	- Each platter side is divided into tracks, and each track is divided into sectors.
	- Each sector stores 512 bytes of data.
	- This structure is called drive geometry or low-level formatting.

- HDD Components
	- Platters – Store magnetic data.
	- Spindle – Spins the platters at high speeds.
	- Read/Write Heads – Magnetically read and write data.
	- Actuator Arm – Moves the heads across the platters.

- Performance Factors
	- Spindle Speed (RPM)
		- Determines how fast platters spin and affects access time.
		- Common speeds:
			- 15,000 RPM → Enterprise-grade, ~180 MB/s transfer rate
			- 10,000 RPM → High-performance
			- 7,200 RPM → Standard desktop drives (~110 MB/s)
			- 5,400 RPM → Low-power or laptop drives

	- Access Time
		- Measured in milliseconds (ms).
		- Includes:
			-  Seek Time: Time for head to move to the correct track.
			- Access Time: Total delay before data can be read.
		- Typical values:
			- High-performance HDD: <3 ms'
			- Average HDD: ~6 ms

	- Data Transfer Rate
		- Measures how fast data moves between the platter and system.
		- Example:
			- 15,000 RPM drive: up to 180 MB/s
			- 7,200 RPM drive: around 110 MB/s

- Interfaces & Form Factors
	- Interface:
		- Most HDDs use the SATA interface (Serial ATA).

	Form Factors:
	Type		Size				Typical Use
	3.5-inch		Larger, faster, more storage	Desktops
	2.5-inch		Smaller, quieter, less power	Laptops & externals

	- 2.5-inch drives can vary in thickness:
		- 15 mm
		- 9.5 mm
		- 7 mm 
		- 5 mm

- Key Takeaways
	- HDDs use mechanical movement → slower than SSDs.
	- RPM and access time determine performance.
	- More fragile due to moving parts.
	- Still widely used for bulk storage because of low cost per GB.

Redundant Array of Independent Disks 3.2.5

- Overview
	- RAID stands for Redundant Array of Independent (or Inexpensive) Disks.
	- Combines multiple HDDs or SSDs into one logical storage unit for better performance, fault tolerance, or both.
	- The OS sees the RAID array as one single drive.

	- Think of RAID like teamwork:
		- If one drive fails, others help keep the data safe and system running.

- Why Use RAID
	- Protects against data loss if a drive fails.
	- Can boost performance by spreading (striping) data across multiple drives.
	- Reduces downtime in case of hardware failure. 
	
	- Risk			Without RAID		With RAID
	Boot Drive Fails		System crashes		System can stay running
	Data Drive Fails		Files lost		Files can be recovered from redundancy

- RAID Implementation
	- RAID can be set up in two main ways:
	- Software RAID
		- Managed by the operating system.
		- Uses the CPU for RAID operations (slightly slower).
		- Less expensive but not as fast or reliable as hardware RAID.
	- Hardware RAID
		- Managed by a dedicated RAID controller (adapter card).
		- Drives connect to the controller’s SATA ports instead of the motherboard.
		- Often supports hot-swapping (replace failed drives without shutting down).
		- Faster and more reliable, but more costly.
	- Some motherboards include built-in RAID support in the chipset.


- Common RAID Levels
RAID Level				Description					Key Benefit
RAID 0 (Striping)								Splits data across drives	 							Best performance ⚠️ No redundancy
RAID 1 (Mirroring)								Duplicates data on two drives	 					Full redundancy ⚠️ Half capacity
RAID 5 (Striping with Parity)					Distributes data + parity across 3+ drives			Good balance of speed + protection
RAID 6 (Double Parity)							Like RAID 5 but with 2 parity blocks					 Survives 2 drive failures
RAID 10 (1+0)									Combines mirroring and striping						High speed + redundancy ⚠️ Expensive (needs 4+ drives)

- Key Terms
	- Fault Tolerance: System keeps working even if a drive fails.
	- Hot-swapping: Replacing a failed drive without turning off the system.
	- Parity: Extra data used to rebuild information if a disk fails.

- Key Takeaways
	- RAID protects data and improves performance by using multiple drives together.
	- RAID 0 = Speed, RAID 1 = Safety, RAID 5/6/10 = Best of both worlds.
	- Hardware RAID = more reliable and supports hot-swapping.

RAID 0 and RAID 1 3.2.6

- Overview
	- When setting up a RAID array, the RAID level determines how data is stored, protected, and accessed.
	- Choosing the right level depends on:
		- Fault tolerance (protection from drive failure)
		- Performance (read/write speed)
		- Capacity (usable storage)
		- Cost (how many drives are needed)
	- Tip: Always use identical drives in size, type, and speed to avoid wasted space or performance bottlenecks.

- RAID 0 – Striping (No Parity)
	- How It Works
		- Splits (stripes) data evenly across two or more drives.
		- Multiple disks can read/write simultaneously, increasing speed.
		- Logical volume = sum of all drive capacities, but limited by smallest drive.

	- Advantages
		- Best read/write performance.
		- Uses 100% of total disk capacity.
		- Great for temporary or non-critical data (like cache or scratch disks).
	
	- Disadvantages
		- No redundancy – if one drive fails, all data is lost.
		- Not suitable for storing important files.

	- Requirements
		- Minimum: 2 drives

- Comparison Summary
Feature				RAID 0 (Striping)		RAID 1 (Mirroring)
Minimum Drives			2			2
Redundancy			None			Full
Performance			Very Fast		Good (faster reads)
Fault Tolerance			None			Survives 1 drive failure
Storage Efficiency		100%			50%
Best For				Gaming, temporary files	Critical data, OS drives

- Key Takeaways
	- RAID 0 = Speed only. No safety.
	- RAID 1 = Safety first. Redundant copy of data.
	- RAID 1 rebuilds quickly, while RAID 0 offers no rebuild option (data lost).
	- Always choose based on importance of data vs. performance needs.
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------
RAID 5 and RAID 10 3.2.7 

- RAID 5 (Striping with Distributed Parity)
	- Minimum Drives: 3
	- How it Works: Data is striped (split up) across all disks like RAID 0, but parity (error correction info) is also stored across all disks.
	- Fault Tolerance: Can survive 1 disk failure — lost data can be rebuilt using parity.
	- Performance:
		- Read speed: Fast (can read from multiple disks).
		- Write speed: Slower (must calculate parity).
	- Storage Efficiency: (n−1)/n — one drive’s worth of space is used for parity.
	- Example: 3 drives of 80 GB → 160 GB usable.
	- Use Case: File servers or environments needing good performance and redundancy.
	- Key Idea: RAID 5 = Fast reads + 1 drive fault tolerance + good capacity efficiency.

- RAID 10 (Stripe of Mirrors)
	- Minimum Drives: 4 (and must be even)
	- How it Works: Combines RAID 1 (mirroring) and RAID 0 (striping):
		- Pairs of disks are mirrored (for safety).
		- Those mirrored pairs are striped (for speed).
	- Fault Tolerance: One disk from each mirror pair can fail — still runs fine.
	- Performance:
		- Read and Write: Very fast (no parity calculation).
	- Storage Efficiency: 50% (half of total disk space used for mirroring).
	- Use Case: Databases or critical systems needing speed and high reliability.
	- Key Idea: RAID 10 = RAID 1’s protection + RAID 0’s speed = fast & safe (but uses 50% of storage).

----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
RAID 6 (Striping with Double Parity) 3.2.8 

- How it Works:
	- Like RAID 5, data is striped across multiple disks.
	- Two sets of parity information are stored (instead of one), allowing the system to rebuild data even if two disks fail.

- Requirements:
	- Minimum drives: 4
	- Usable storage: Total capacity minus the size of two disks (because two are used for parity).
		- Example: 4 × 1 TB drives → 2 TB usable space
- Performance:
	- Read speed: Good (similar to RAID 5)
	- Write speed: Slower (because two parity sets must be calculated and written)

- Fault Tolerance:
	- Can survive two simultaneous disk failures — great for large or mission-critical systems.

- pros:
	- Very high fault tolerance
	- Great for big storage arrays
	- Keeps working even after one or two disk failures
- cons: 
	- Slower writes (extra parity work)
	- More expensive (needs extra disks + stronger controller)
	- Longer rebuild times

- Use Case:
	- Enterprise servers, data centers, or systems that cannot afford downtime or data loss.

- Quick Tip to Remember:
	- RAID 6 = RAID 5 + Extra Parity = Survives 2 Disk Failures

System RAM and Virtual Memory 3.3.1

- CPU Memory Hierarchy:
	- Registers → Cache → RAM → Storage (SSD/HDD)
	- CPU cache is very fast but small, so RAM supports it by storing active programs and data.

- System RAM (Random Access Memory)
	- Type: Volatile memory → loses data when power is off.
	- Speed: Faster than SSDs, much faster than HDDs.
	- Capacity: Measured in GB → affects:
		- Number of applications you can run simultaneously
		- Ability to work with large files efficiently
	- Process Execution:
		- Program/data loaded from fixed disk → RAM
		- CPU fetches instructions from RAM → Cache → Registers

- Memory Bus: Data and Address Pathways
	Pathway		Function
	Data Bus		Transfers information per clock cycle (typical: 64-bit for single channel)
	Address Bus	Tracks memory locations → limits max memory CPU can access
  
	- 32-bit CPU: 32-bit address bus → max 4 GB memory
	- 64-bit CPU:
		- Theoretical: 64-bit address space → 16 exabytes
		- Practical: Most use 48-bit address bus → max 256 TB memory
- Key Points
	- 64-bit CPUs can address more memory locations than 32-bit CPUs.
	- Data bus width (e.g., 64-bit) determines how much memory can be transferred per cycle.
	- RAM is temporary memory for running programs; storage (HDD/SSD) is permanent memory.

- Quick Tip for Exam:
	- Think of it like a pipeline: Storage feeds RAM → RAM feeds CPU cache → CPU executes instructions. 
	- More RAM = smoother multitasking and faster program execution.

RAM Types 3.3.2

-Modern RAM is mostly DDR SDRAM (Double Data Rate Synchronous Dynamic RAM). RAM evolution:
-  DRAM (Dynamic RAM):
	- Stores bits as electrical charges in capacitors.
	- 1 = charged, 0 = not charged.
-  SDRAM (Synchronous DRAM):
	- Synchronized to the system clock, so memory operations align with CPU instructions.
- DDR SDRAM (Double Data Rate SDRAM):
	- Transfers data on both rising and falling clock edges → doubles data rate.
	- Modules labeled by peak transfer rate (e.g., PC1600, PC2100).

- DDR Calculation Example (DDR-200 / PC1600)
Step			Explanation
Clock Speed		100 MHz (memory bus & internal clock)
Data Rate		2 operations per cycle → 200 MT/s (megatransfers/sec)
Peak Transfer Rate	200 MT/s × 8 bytes/transfer = 1600 MB/s → PC1600



-DDR Generations Comparison
Type	Data Rate (MT/s)		Transfer Rate (GB/s)	Max Module Size
DDR1	200–400			1.6–3.2			1 GB
DDR2	400–1066			3.2–8.5			4 GB
DDR3	800–2133			6.4–17.066		16 GB
DDR4	1600–3200		12.8–25.6			32 GB
DDR5	4800–8000+		38.4–51.2+		128 GB+

	- MT/s: Megatransfers per second → millions of data transfers per second.
	- Transfer rate: Speed at which data is transferred by the memory controller.

- Timing and Latency
	- RAM modules have internal timings, e.g., 14-15-15-35 CAS 14.
	- CAS latency: Delay (in clock cycles) between memory controller request and data availability.
		- Lower CAS latency = faster access.
	- Important when comparing modules of the same type and speed.

- Architectural Improvements
	- DDR4:
		- Higher density → larger capacities.
		- Improved power efficiency and channel design → better throughput.
	- DDR5:
		- Dual 32-bit subchannel per DIMM → more efficient access.
		- Reduced latency and higher power efficiency.
- Quick Exam Tips:
	- DDR = Double Data Rate → 2 transfers per clock cycle.
	- DDR5 > DDR4 > DDR3 in speed, capacity, and efficiency.
	- Always check CAS latency for performance comparison.

	- Modules with heat sinks = high-speed / high-performance RAM.

Bandwidth		Standard			Doubled

	- Dual-channel DDR5 → 4 × 32-bit channels, distributing memory load more efficiently.
	- Benefits: higher density per module, lower latency, better utilization for multi-core CPUs.

CPU Architecture 3.4.1

- Definition:
	-  The CPU (Central Processing Unit) — or processor — is the computer’s main component that executes program instructions. Every running program (OS, app, or firmware) is converted into machine instructions that the CPU understands.

- CPU Instruction Cycle
	- Fetch:
		- The control unit retrieves the next instruction from system memory into the CPU’s pipeline.
	- Decode:
		- The control unit interprets the instruction and decides what needs to be done.
		- It sends the instruction to either the ALU (Arithmetic Logic Unit) or FPU (Floating Point Unit) if math is required.
	- Execute:
		- The ALU or FPU performs the actual operation (e.g., calculations, logic comparisons).
	- Write-Back:
		- The result is saved to a register, cache, or system memory for later use.

- CPU Components
	- Register:
		- Tiny, ultra-fast storage areas inside the CPU.
		- Operate at the same clock speed as the CPU.
		- Store temporary data during processing.
	- Cache:
		- Small, high-speed memory located on or near the CPU.
		- Stores frequently used data and instructions to reduce access time to slower system RAM.
		- Levels:
			- L1 Cache: Fastest, smallest (inside the CPU core).
			- L2 Cache: Larger, slightly slower.
			- L3 Cache: Shared between cores, largest and slowest of the three.

- Key Point
	- Registers and cache help the CPU work faster by reducing delays caused by fetching data from slower system memory.

x86 CPU Architecture 3.4.2

- Overview:
	- CPU architecture determines how a processor handles instructions, affecting speed, efficiency, and performance.
	- There are two main types of architectures

RISC vs. CISC
Feature			RISC (Reduced Instruction Set Computing)					CISC (Complex Instruction Set Computing)
Instruction Set		Small, simple, and optimized						Large and complex
Execution		Faster, uses fewer cycles per instruction					Slower per instruction, but can do more per instruction
Best For			Power-efficient and high-speed systems (phones, tablets, embedded devices)	General-purpose computing (PCs, laptops, servers)
Example			ARM architecture (used in mobile devices)					x86 architecture (used in desktops and servers)



- x86 Architecture (CISC Design)
	- Used in most desktop and server CPUs.
	- Supports:
		- 32-bit (IA-32) and
		- 64-bit (x86-64) instruction sets.
	- Developed mainly by Intel and AMD.

- Core CPU Components
	- ALU (Arithmetic Logic Unit): Performs math and logic operations.
	- Control Unit: Manages the fetch → decode → execute cycle.
	- Cache Memory: Small, high-speed memory built into the CPU.
		- L1 Cache: Fastest, smallest, inside each core.
		- L2 Cache: Larger, slower, per core.
		- L3 Cache: Shared among cores, largest, slower than L1/L2.

- Performance Features
	- Multi-Core Processors:
		- Contain multiple processing cores on a single chip.
		- Enable parallel processing (doing multiple tasks at once).
		- Great for multitasking, gaming, and content creation.
	- Hyper-Threading (Intel) / SMT (AMD):
		- Allows each core to handle two threads at once.
		- Simulates additional cores for improved efficiency and responsiveness.

- CPU Examples
	- Intel Core Series (i3, i5, i7, i9)
	- AMD Ryzen Series (3, 5, 7, 9)
	- These CPUs use multi-core and hyper-threading features for high performance in gaming, video editing, and data-heavy tasks.

- Key Takeaway
	- RISC: Efficiency and speed → mobile and embedded systems.
	- CISC/x86: Flexibility and performance → desktops and servers.
	- Understanding these differences helps choose the right CPU for specific workloads.

x64 CPU Architecture 3.4.3


- Overview:
	- The x64 (x86-64) architecture is the 64-bit extension of the older x86 (32-bit) architecture.
	- It was developed by AMD (as AMD64) and later adopted by Intel (as Intel 64 or EM64T).

- x86 vs. x64 Overview

Software Support		Runs only 32-bit software		Runs both 32-bit and 64-bit software
Typical Use		Older systems (1990s–2010s)	Modern PCs, servers, and laptops


- Key Features of x64 Architecture
	- 64-bit instructions, data paths, and memory addressing
		- Enables larger amounts of RAM and faster data handling.
	- Backward Compatibility:
		- Can run 32-bit programs, but 32-bit CPUs cannot run 64-bit software.
	- Software Requirements:
		- OS, drivers, and apps must be compiled for 64-bit use.
		- Device drivers must match OS architecture
			- 64-bit OS requires 64-bit drivers.

	- Operating Systems:
		- Windows 11 (64-bit only)
		- Most modern Linux distributions (64-bit)
		- macOS (64-bit since Catalina — dropped 32-bit support)
	- Mobile Example:
		- Apple’s A7 chip (iPhone 5s) started the 64-bit transition.
	- Applications:
		- Adobe Creative Cloud and similar apps are now 64-bit for better performance and memory handling.


- Performance & Security Benefits
	- Better performance: Handles larger data sets and multitasking more efficiently.
	- More memory capacity: Supports more than 4 GB of RAM.
	- Virtualization Support:
		- Intel VT-x and AMD-V technologies improve virtual machine performance.
	- Enhanced Security:
		- Data Execution Prevention (DEP) — blocks code from running in non-executable memory regions.

- Key Term
	- Device Driver: Software that allows the operating system to communicate with hardware components.
		- Must match the OS architecture (64-bit OS → 64-bit driver).

- Quick Summary
	- x64 = 64-bit version of x86.	
	- Supports more RAM, higher performance, 64-bit virtualization, and better security.
	- Now the standard for all modern computers.

ARM CPU Architecture 3.4.4

- What it is:
	- ARM = Advanced RISC Machines — CPU designs licensed to companies like Apple, Qualcomm, Nvidia, and Samsung.
	- Used in mobile devices, Apple M1/M2 Macs, Chromebooks, and some Windows tablets/laptops.
	- Often designed as a System-on-Chip (SoC) — CPU + GPU + memory + networking + storage controller in one chip.

- Key Characteristics
	- Based on RISC (Reduced Instruction Set Computing) → simpler, faster instructions → high efficiency.
	- Compared to x86/x64 (CISC), ARM uses less power and generates less heat.
	- Ideal for mobile and fanless systems due to low power use and small size.

- Software Compatibility
	- OS, drivers, and apps must be compiled for ARM to run natively.
	- Emulation allows ARM devices to run x86/x64 software (e.g., Windows on ARM, Apple Rosetta 2),
but this usually comes with a performance penalty.
	- Example:
		- iOS/macOS optimized for ARM.
		- Android built mainly for ARM.
		- Windows 10/11 ARM can emulate x86/x64 programs.

- Advantages
	- Power-efficient → longer battery life.
	- Low heat output → allows passive cooling (no fans).
	- Integrated design → cheaper, thinner, and lighter devices.
	- High performance per watt (especially Apple’s M1/M2).

- Physical Design
	- ARM SoCs are soldered to the motherboard (non-replaceable).
	- Integration reduces space, cost, and need for extra components.
	- Used in fanless laptops, phones, tablets, and other compact devices.

CPU Features 3.4.5

- Clock Speed
	- Clock speed = how fast a CPU executes instructions (measured in GHz).
	- Good for comparing CPUs of the same architecture, but not reliable across different ones (e.g., ARM vs x64).
	- Performance limited by power and heat — CPUs can’t just keep getting faster.
	- Efficiency improved by optimizing pipelines to do more work per clock cycle.

- Multithreading (SMT / Hyper-Threading)
	- Allows one physical core to handle multiple instruction streams (threads).
	- Reduces CPU idle time → boosts performance for multithreaded apps.
	- Examples:
		- Intel Hyper-Threading (HT)
		- AMD Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT)
	- Example: A 4-core CPU with SMT appears as 8 logical cores (4C/8T).
	- Used in tasks like video editing, gaming, and virtualization.

- Symmetric Multiprocessing (SMP)
	- Uses 2+ physical CPUs in one system.
	- OS distributes work across all CPUs automatically.
	- Common in servers and high-end workstations.
	- Requires:
		- Multisocket motherboard
		- CPUs of the same model/specifications
		- SMP-capable operating system

- Chip-Level Multiprocessing (CMP / Multicore CPUs)
	- Multiple cores built onto one physical CPU chip.
	- Each core = its own execution unit + cache.
	- Shared caches often used for efficiency.
	- Simplifies design vs. SMP (no multiple sockets needed).
	- Common notations:
		- 8C/16T → 8 cores, 16 threads (supports multithreading).
	- Now standard in desktops, laptops, and servers.

- Virtualization Support
	- Enables multiple operating systems (VMs) to run on one machine.
	- Look for CPUs with hardware virtualization:
		- Intel VT-x
		- AMD-V
	- These make VMs run smoother and faster by offloading work from software to hardware.

- Advanced Virtualization Features
	- SLAT (Second Level Address Translation):
		- Intel: Extended Page Table (EPT)
		- AMD: Rapid Virtualization Indexing (RVI)
		- Improves memory management for VMs → faster performance.
	- IOMMU (Input-Output Memory Management Unit):
		- Helps manage hardware access by VMs.
		- Improves security and performance.
	- Ideal virtualization CPUs:
		- High core count
		- Multithreading support (HT/SMT)
		- SLAT and IOMMU support

- Quick Recap Table
Feature			Description				Example
Clock Speed		Measures CPU speed (GHz)			4.0 GHz
SMT / Hyper-Threading	1 core = multiple threads			Intel HT, AMD SMT
SMP			Multiple CPUs in one system		High-end servers
CMP / Multicore		Multiple cores on one chip			8C/16T CPU
Virtualization		Run multiple OSs				Intel VT-x, AMD-V
SLAT			Boosts VM memory efficiency		Intel EPT, AMD RVI

3.4.6
CPU Socket Types

- What is a CPU Socket?
	- The CPU socket connects the processor to the motherboard.
	- Each CPU brand (Intel or AMD) has its own socket type — they are not interchangeable.
	- ZIF (Zero Insertion Force) mechanism lets you install the CPU without pressure, preventing pin damage.
	- Tip: Always use anti-ESD precautions (wear a wrist strap or ground yourself) when handling CPUs. They are sensitive to static electricity.

- Intel Socket Types
	- Intel uses LGA (Land Grid Array) sockets.
		- Pins are on the motherboard socket.
		- CPU has flat contact pads.

- Common Intel Sockets:
Socket		Description				CPU Generation
LGA 1200		Used for Intel 10th & 11th Gen Core CPUs	Comet Lake and Rocket Lake
LGA 1700		Used for Intel 12th Gen and newer CPUs	Alder Lake and later

- Intel Feature:	
	- Turbo Boost — automatically increases CPU speed during heavy workloads.
Intel Feature:
Turbo Boost — automatically increases CPU speed during heavy workloads.

- Handling Tips (Intel or AMD):
	- Always align Pin 1 on the CPU with Pin 1 on the socket.
	- When removing a CPU attached to a heat sink:
		- Gently twist the heat sink to loosen it.
		- Do not pull straight up — it can rip the CPU out of the socket.
		- Unlock the latch before removing the CPU.
	- When reinstalling:
		- Clean old thermal paste off both surfaces.
		- Apply a small amount of new thermal paste (pea-sized or "X" pattern).
		- Too much paste = bad heat transfer or possible damage.

- AMD Socket Types
	- AMD mostly uses PGA (Pin Grid Array) sockets.
		- Pins are on the CPU, and the holes are on the motherboard.
	- However, some high-end AMD CPUs (like servers) use LGA instead.

- Common AMD Sockets:
Socket	Description				Used For
AM4	Most common for Ryzen desktop CPUs		Mainstream desktops
TR4	For Threadripper CPUs			High-performance desktops
SP3	Uses LGA design for EPYC CPUs		Servers and data centers


- LGA vs PGA Summary
Feature			Intel (LGA)				AMD (PGA)
Pin Location		On motherboard socket			On CPU itself
Durability		Less risk to CPU pins			Easier to bend pins
Used For			Desktop & server CPUs (LGA 1200, 1700)	Ryzen (AM4), Threadripper (TR4), EPYC (SP3)
Installation		ZIF lever + contact pads			ZIF lever + insert pins

- Key Takeaways
	- Intel → LGA sockets (pins on motherboard).
	- AMD → PGA sockets (pins on CPU) — except SP3 (LGA).
	- Always match CPU and motherboard socket types.
	- Use thermal paste and anti-static precautions during installation.
	- ZIF sockets prevent damage by eliminating force during installation.

3.4.7
CPU Types and Motherboard Compatibility

-Overview
	- CPUs are constantly being updated — new generations = new sockets + new chipsets.
	- Intel and AMD use different socket designs — you can’t mix brands.
	- Compatibility depends on:
		- Socket type (physical fit)
		- Chipset support (features and firmware)
		- CPU generation (matching BIOS and architecture)
	-  Even if a CPU fits physically, it may not work unless the chipset and BIOS support it.

- Chipsets and Motherboard Features
	- The chipset controls what features the motherboard supports:
	- Overclocking
	- PCIe lanes
	- Memory type and speed

Brand	High-End Chipset		Budget Chipset		Notes
Intel	Z-Series (Z690, Z790)	B- and H-Series		Z-series allows overclocking
AMD	X-Series (X670)		B-Series, A-Series	X-series = more features, PCIe lanes

Form Factors affect how many components you can install:

Form Factor		Size		Typical Use
ATX			Full-size	Most expansion slots and RAM slots
MicroATX		Medium		Fewer expansion slots
Mini-ITX			Small		Compact builds (1 GPU slot, 2 RAM slots)


- Installing a CPU and Motherboard
	- Socket Alignment → Match CPU and socket (e.g., LGA for Intel, AM4/AM5 for AMD).
		- Use notches or triangle markers for correct orientation.
	- Secure CPU → Place it gently in the socket. Lock it with the ZIF lever (no force).
	- Apply Thermal Paste → Small pea-sized amount in center of CPU.
	- Attach Cooler → Align and tighten evenly. Connect cooler to CPU fan header.
	- Install Motherboard → Align with standoffs in the case and secure with screws.
	-  If you remove a CPU cooler, twist gently to loosen — don’t pull straight up.

- Upgrading CPUs
	- Upgrades are limited — new CPUs often need new sockets/chipsets.
	- Even if compatible, upgrades might not give major performance gains without updating other components (RAM, motherboard, GPU).



- Core Configurations and Performance
Term			Description			Benefit
Single-Core		One core = one task at a time	Simpler tasks
Multi-Core		Multiple cores = multiple tasks	Better multitasking
Hyper-Threading / SMT	Each core runs 2 threads		More efficiency in parallel tasks

- Examples:
	- Gaming: Needs high single-core speed → Intel i5 / AMD Ryzen 5
	- Virtualization: Needs many cores + SMT → Ryzen 9 / Threadripper / Core i9
	- Workstations: Needs many cores + threads → AMD Threadripper / Intel Xeon

- Desktop CPU Families
Brand	CPU Tiers					Socket				Notes

AMD	Ryzen 3 → 5 → 7 → 9 → Threadripper			AM4 → AM5			AM5 adds DDR5 + PCIe 5.0; AM5 = LGA style

- Workstations
	- Used for video editing, 3D rendering, and software development.
	- Similar components to servers, but for desktop environments.
	- Use Threadripper (AMD) or Xeon (Intel) CPUs.

- Servers
	- Designed for high reliability and multitasking.
	- Support multiple CPU sockets and ECC memory (error-correcting RAM).
	- Use server-grade motherboards with advanced cooling and scalability.

Brand		Server CPU		Socket
Intel Xeon	Xeon Scalable		LGA 4189, older LGA 3647
AMD EPYC		EPYC Genoa / Bergamo	SP3, newer SP5

- Mobile Devices
	- Prioritize battery life and cooling over raw power.
	- Use ARM-based CPUs (phones/tablets) or mobile variants of desktop chips (e.g., Intel Core i7 Mobile, AMD Ryzen Mobile).
	- Mobile CPUs are often soldered to the motherboard — not upgradeable.

- Quick Comparison Chart
Category	Typical CPU		Socket		Upgradeable?

Workstation	Xeon / Threadripper	LGA 4189 / TR4	Usually

- Key Takeaways
	- Always match CPU, socket, and chipset.
	- Z-series (Intel) and X-series (AMD) = high-end boards with overclocking.
	- AM5 and LGA 1700 are current mainstream sockets.
	- Multi-core + SMT = best for multitasking and virtualization.
	- Mobile CPUs trade power for efficiency and are non-replaceable.
