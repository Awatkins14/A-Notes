## **What Is a Network Type?**

A **network type** categorizes a network based on the **geographic area it covers** and how it is managed.

Choosing the correct network type helps with:

* Installation planning
* Support procedures
* Cost management
* Security planning

---

# **Common Network Types**

---

## **PAN (Personal Area Network)**

**Definition:**
A small network centered around one person.

**Range:** A few meters

**Examples:**

* Bluetooth smartwatch + phone
* Wireless earbuds + phone
* Laptop + Bluetooth mouse

✅ **Exam Answer:**
Smartwatch + earbuds connected via Bluetooth = **PAN**

---

## **LAN (Local Area Network)**

**Definition:**
A network covering a small area such as:

* Home
* Office
* School building

**Characteristics:**

* High speed
* Privately managed
* Usually Ethernet or Wi-Fi based

Most wired LANs are based on:

* **IEEE 802.3 (Ethernet standard)**

✅ **Exam Answer:**
Most cabled LANs use **IEEE 802.3**

---

## **WLAN (Wireless LAN)**

**Definition:**
A LAN that uses wireless technology instead of cables.

Based on:

* **IEEE 802.11 (Wi-Fi standard)**

Used in:

* Homes
* Offices
* Coffee shops

---

## **MAN (Metropolitan Area Network)**

**Definition:**
A network that covers the size of a city.

Larger than a LAN, smaller than a WAN.

✅ **Exam Answer:**
A network covering a city = **MAN**

---

## **WAN (Wide Area Network)**

**Definition:**
A network that spans:

* Large geographic areas
* Multiple cities
* Countries
* Continents

Example:

* The Internet
* Corporate networks connecting multiple offices

If a small business has multiple offices across an area, they likely need a:

* **WAN**

---

## **SAN (Storage Area Network)**

**Definition:**
A dedicated network for storage devices.

Purpose:

* Provides access to consolidated storage
* Used in enterprise environments

---

## **CAN (Campus Area Network)**

**Definition:**
Connects multiple LANs across a limited campus area.

Used by:

* Universities
* Large corporate campuses

---

## **Data Center**

A **whole site dedicated to provisioning server resources** is called a:

✅ **Data Center**

Purpose:

* Hosts servers
* Provides cloud services
* Stores enterprise data

---

# **Modular Network Design**

If you create a network where each segment is designed as a **modular function**, you are using:

* **Software-Defined Networking (SDN)** concepts
  or
* A **modular network architecture**

This allows:

* Scalability
* Easier management
* Logical segmentation

---

# **Quick Exam Answers (Learning Outcomes)**

1. Network covering a city → **MAN**
2. Smartwatch + earbuds via Bluetooth → **PAN**
3. Network built with modular segments → **Modular architecture / SDN**
4. Whole site for server provisioning → **Data Center**
5. Most cabled LANs based on → **IEEE 802.3**

5.1.2 SOHO and Enterprise Networks

---

# **SOHO Networks**

## **Definition**

A **SOHO (Small Office/Home Office)** network is a small LAN designed for:

* Home users
* Small businesses
* Small number of users

---

## **Typical Characteristics**

* May use:

  * A small server
  * Or just client devices + printers
* Usually relies on a single device called a:

  * SOHO router
  * Internet router
  * Broadband router

---

## **SOHO Router Functions**

A SOHO router typically combines:

* Router
* Switch
* Wireless Access Point (AP)
* Firewall
* DHCP server
* NAT

All-in-one device.

---

## **Design Focus**

* Low cost
* Easy setup
* Basic security
* Supports small number of users

---

# **Enterprise Networks**

## **Definition**

An **Enterprise network** supports:

* Large businesses
* Universities
* Government institutions
* Hundreds or thousands of users

Unlike SOHO networks:

* Each network function is handled by a **separate device**
* Designed for high reliability and scalability

---

# **Enterprise Network Design (Modular Architecture)**

Enterprise LANs are built in **modular segments**.

Each part has a dedicated function.

---

## **1. Access Layer (Work Areas)**

* Client PCs
* Printers
* Connected via:

  * Workgroup switches
  * Structured cabling

Wireless devices connect through:

* Wireless Access Points (APs)

---

## **2. Distribution/Core Layer**

* Core switches
* Distribution switches
* Routers
* Firewalls

Responsible for:

* Routing traffic
* Managing VLANs
* Controlling access
* High-speed backbone connectivity

---

## **3. Server Room / Data Center**

* Servers separated from clients
* Controlled access
* Centralized storage and services

---

# **Screened Subnets (DMZ)**

Internet-facing services are placed in a protected area called a:

* **Screened subnet**
* Also known as a **DMZ (Demilitarized Zone)**

---

## **Purpose of Screened Subnet**

* Sits between:

  * Private LAN
  * Public Internet
* Traffic is:

  * Strictly filtered
  * Monitored

---

## **Border Services in Enterprise Networks**

Provide:

* Internet access
* Email services
* Remote access
* WAN branch office connectivity
* VPN connections
* Public web services

---

# **Purpose of Networking Hardware**

Networking hardware allows computers and devices to **connect, communicate, and share resources** over a network.

When setting up a network, you’ll need to identify the correct hardware to match the network type.

---

# **1. Network Interface Card (NIC)**

* A **NIC** is the computer component responsible for establishing the **physical connection** to a network.
* Can be **wired** (Ethernet) or **wireless** (Wi-Fi).
* Provides the computer with a **MAC address** (unique identifier).

**MAC Address Note:**

* The first 24 bits of a MAC address are the **Organizationally Unique Identifier (OUI)**, identifying the manufacturer.
* The last 24 bits are a unique number assigned by the manufacturer.

---

# **2. Cables and Connectors**

* Network cables run from wall ports to devices and are **terminated with connectors**:

  * **RJ-45** for Ethernet
  * **RJ-11** for telephone lines

* Proper termination ensures reliable connections and prevents signal loss.

---

# **3. Switches**

* Switches connect devices within a **LAN**, forwarding traffic based on **MAC addresses**.

### **Types of Switches**

1. **Unmanaged Switch**

   * Works **out of the box** without configuration
   * Good for **small networks** or plug-and-play setups

2. **Managed Switch**

   * Configurable for advanced features: VLANs, QoS, SNMP
   * Suitable for **enterprise networks**

---

# **4. Power over Ethernet (PoE)**

* PoE delivers **electrical power through Ethernet cables** to devices like phones, cameras, and APs.

**Standards:**

* **IEEE 802.3af** → 15.4W
* **IEEE 802.3at (PoE+)** → 25.5W
* **IEEE 802.3bt (PoE++ / 4PPoE)** → ~51W (high-power devices)

# **5.2.1 Network Interface Cards (NICs)**

## **Purpose of a NIC**

* A **Network Interface Card (NIC)** establishes the **physical connection** between a computer and a network.

* Can use:

  * **Copper twisted pair cables** (e.g., 1000BASE-T)
  * **Fiber optic cables** (light pulses for high-speed transmission)

* Most modern motherboards have a **built-in Gigabit Ethernet adapter**.

* Expansion NICs can:

  * Support different media (fiber, higher-speed Ethernet)
  * Provide multiple ports for **link aggregation** (e.g., 4 × 1 Gbps → 4 Gbps combined)

---

## **How NICs Work**

* Converts electrical or optical signals into **digital data**.

* **Framing and addressing** are handled by the Ethernet data link protocol.

* Every NIC has a **unique MAC address** that identifies the **source and destination** of Ethernet frames.

---

## **MAC Address Structure**

* **Size:** 48 bits (6 bytes)
* **Representation:** 12 hexadecimal digits (0–9, A–F), usually separated by colons or hyphens:

  * Example: `00:60:8C:12:3A:BC` or `00608C123ABC`

### **MAC Address Breakdown**

1. **Organizationally Unique Identifier (OUI)** – first 24 bits

   * Identifies the **manufacturer** of the NIC.
2. **NIC-Specific Identifier** – last 24 bits

   * Unique for each NIC

### **Special Flags in MAC**

* First two hex digits converted to binary: last two bits are flags:

  1. **Individual vs. Group/Multicast** – last bit: 0 = individual, 1 = multicast
  2. **Universally vs. Locally Administered** – second-to-last bit: 0 = globally unique, 1 = locally assigned

**Example:**

* MAC `01:13:10:6B:17:A8` → `01` = `00000001`

  * Last bit = 1 → multicast
  * Next bit = 0 → globally unique

5.2.3 Patch Panels

## **Purpose**

* Patch panels are **central connection points** for structured cabling in offices or enterprise networks.
* They organize and terminate **cables running through walls** from wall ports.

---

## **Connections**

1. **Back of the Patch Panel:**

   * Uses **Insulation Displacement Connector (IDC) punchdown blocks** to terminate cables.
   * Wires are pressed into the IDC without stripping insulation.

2. **Front of the Patch Panel:**

   * Pre-wired **RJ45 ports** provide connection points.
   * **Patch cords** connect the patch panel ports to **switch ports**.

---

## **Advantages**

* Simplifies changing network connections without rerunning cables.
* Centralizes network management for easier troubleshooting and maintenance.

5.2.6 Switches

## **Purpose**

* Connects **multiple devices** within a LAN.
* Provides **one dedicated port per device**.

---

## **How Switches Work**

1. **MAC Address Table:**

   * Records which MAC addresses are on which ports.
   * Helps forward frames **only to the correct port**, reducing unnecessary traffic.

2. **Frame Forwarding:**

   * Reads the **source and destination MAC** of incoming frames.
   * If the destination MAC is unknown, the frame is **flooded to all ports**.

3. **Collision Domains:**

   * Each port is a **separate collision domain**.
   * Full-duplex connections allow **simultaneous send and receive** at maximum network speed.

5.2.9 Unmanaged vs Managed Switches

## **Unmanaged Switch**

* Works **out-of-the-box** with **no configuration needed**.
* Connect devices and it establishes **Ethernet connectivity automatically**.
* Typically has **4–8 ports** for **small networks or SOHO setups**.
* Example: the built-in 4-port switch in most ISP-provided routers.

---

## **Managed Switch**

* Designed for **larger LANs and enterprise networks**.
* Works like an unmanaged switch initially but allows **administrator configuration**:

  * Security settings
  * VLANs, port management, traffic monitoring, etc.
* Typically **24–48 ports** for workgroup clients, servers, and printers.
* Includes **uplink ports** to connect to other switches.
* Can be **configured via web GUI or command-line interface (CLI)**.

---

## **Modular Switch**

* Enterprise-grade, chassis-based switches.
* Interconnect **multiple switch units** with a **shared backplane and power supply**.
* Can provide **hundreds of access ports** from a single modular system.

*5.2.10 – Power over Ethernet (PoE)

---

# **5.2.10 Power over Ethernet (PoE)**

PoE allows a **network switch to provide electrical power over standard Ethernet cabling** to devices such as VoIP phones, wireless access points, and IP cameras.

---

## **IEEE PoE Standards**

| Standard    | Type                    | Max Power (usable)           | Typical Devices                                                          |
| ----------- | ----------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **802.3af** | Type 1 (2-pair PoE)     | ~13 W                        | VoIP phones, basic APs, basic cameras                                    |
| **802.3at** | Type 2 (PoE+)           | ~25 W                        | Advanced APs, PTZ cameras, video IP phones                               |
| **802.3bt** | Type 3 / Type 4 (PoE++) | Type 3: 51 W<br>Type 4: 73 W | LED lighting, digital signage, point-of-sale systems, high-power devices |

---

## **How PoE Works**

1. **Detection Phase:** The switch detects if the connected device supports PoE.
2. **Power Allocation:** Determines required power and sets the voltage appropriately.
3. **Non-PoE Safety:** If the device is non-PoE, no power is sent, preventing damage.

---

## **PoE Devices**

* **Endspan (PSE) Switch:** A PoE-enabled switch that supplies power directly.
* **PoE Injector (Midspan):** Adds PoE capability to a non-PoE switch.

  * Connects between switch and device.
  * Maximum total cable length: 100 m (328 ft).

---

## **Benefits of PoE**

* Eliminates need for separate AC adapters.
* Centralized power management and energy-saving schemes (e.g., sleep states, power capping).
* Simplifies installation of networked devices in hard-to-reach locations.

5.3.1 Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP)

## **What It Is**

* The **most common type of network cable**.
* Made of **copper wiring**.
* Contains **4 pairs of wires** (8 total wires).

---

## **How It’s Built**

* Each pair of wires is:

  * **Twisted together**
  * Twisted at **different rates** from other pairs
* Twisting reduces **electromagnetic interference (EMI)**.
* Uses **balanced signaling**:

  * One wire carries a positive signal.
  * The other carries an equal but opposite signal.
  * Helps cancel out interference.

---

## **Limitations**

* Signal weakens over distance (**attenuation**).
* Maximum recommended length:
  **100 meters (328 feet)** per cable run.

---

## **Why It’s Popular**

* Affordable
* Easy to install
* Reliable for standard LAN distances

------------------------------------------------------------------------

5.3.2 Shielded Twisted Pair (STP)

## **What It Is**

* Twisted pair cable with **extra shielding** to protect against interference.
* Used in environments with **high electromagnetic interference (EMI)**:

  * Near fluorescent lights
  * Power lines
  * Motors
  * Generators

---

## **Types of Shielded Cable**

### **F/UTP (Foiled Unshielded Twisted Pair)**

* Single **foil shield** around all wire pairs.
* Also called:

  * ScTP (Screened Twisted Pair)
  * FTP (Foiled Twisted Pair)
* ✔ Good EMI protection
* ✔ Moderate cost

---

### **S/FTP (Shielded Foiled Twisted Pair)**

* **Braided outer screen**
* Each pair also has **foil shielding**
* ✔ Best protection against EMI and crosstalk
* ✖ More expensive
* ✖ Less flexible
* Variant: **F/FTP** (foil outer shield + foil pairs)

---

### **U/FTP (Unshielded with Foiled Twisted Pair)**

* No outer shield
* Each pair individually foil shielded
* ✔ Good EMI and crosstalk protection

---

## **Important Installation Note**

* Shielding **must be bonded (grounded) properly**.
* If not grounded, shielding can act like an **antenna** and cause interference.
* Modern cables, connectors, and patch panels support proper bonding.

---

## 🔑 Exam Comparison: UTP vs STP

| Feature        | UTP           | STP            |
| -------------- | ------------- | -------------- |
| Shielding      | No            | Yes            |
| Cost           | Cheaper       | More expensive |
| Flexibility    | More flexible | Less flexible  |
| EMI Protection | Basic         | High           |
| Max Distance   | 100m          | 100m           |

5.3.4 Cat (Category) Standards

A **Category (Cat)** rating defines the **performance level** of twisted-pair cabling.
Higher Cat number = **higher speed capability**.

Defined by: **TIA/EIA-568-C** cabling standards.

---

# 📊 Cat Cable Comparison Chart

| Cat        | Max Speed                        | Max Distance | Ethernet Standard          |
| ---------- | -------------------------------- | ------------ | -------------------------- |
| **Cat 5**  | 100 Mbps                         | 100 m        | 100BASE-TX (Fast Ethernet) |
| **Cat 5e** | 1 Gbps                           | 100 m        | 1000BASE-T (Gigabit)       |
| **Cat 6**  | 1 Gbps (100m)<br>10 Gbps (55m)   | 100 m / 55 m | 1000BASE-T / 10GBASE-T     |
| **Cat 6A** | 10 Gbps                          | 100 m        | 10GBASE-T                  |
| **Cat 7**  | 10 Gbps (100m)<br>100 Gbps (15m) | 100 m / 15 m | 10GBASE-T / 100GBASE-T     |
| **Cat 8**  | 25–40 Gbps                       | 30 m         | 25GBASE-T / 40GBASE-T      |

---

# 🧠 Important Exam Details

## **Cat 5**

* Supports only **100 Mbps**
* Obsolete (no longer sold)
* Must be replaced for Gigabit networks

---

## **Cat 5e**

* Supports **1 Gbps at 100m**
* Still acceptable for most home/client networks

---

## **Cat 6**

* More reliable than 5e
* Supports:

  * 1 Gbps up to 100m
  * 10 Gbps up to 55m
* Very common today

---

## **Cat 6A**

* Supports **10 Gbps at full 100m**
* Thicker and harder to install
* Recommended for:

  * Healthcare
  * 802.3bt (PoE++)
  * Wireless AP backhaul

---

## **Cat 7**

* Mainly used in **data centers**
* Uses **GG45 or TERA connectors**
* Not widely used in homes/offices
* RJ45 may not support full Cat 7 performance

---

## **Cat 8**

* Designed for **short, high-speed data center runs**
* Max length: **30 meters**
* Two types:

  * **Cat 8.1** → uses RJ45 (backward compatible)
  * **Cat 8.2** → uses GG45/TERA (not backward compatible)

5.3.5 Copper Cabling Connectors

## **RJ45 (8P8C) Connector**

* Used for **Ethernet twisted-pair cabling**
* Also called **8P8C**

  * 8 Positions
  * 8 Contacts
* Used with **4-pair Ethernet cable (8 wires total)**

---

## **Wire Color Coding**

Each twisted pair has a color:

* 🟠 Orange
* 🟢 Green
* 🔵 Blue
* 🟤 Brown

Each pair contains:

* One **white wire with color stripe**
* One **solid color wire**

Example:

* White/Orange + Orange
* White/Green + Green

---

# **T568A vs T568B Wiring Standards**

Defined by **TIA/EIA-568**

## **T568A**

* Pin 1 → White/Green
* Pin 2 → Green
* Pin 3 → White/Orange
* Pin 6 → Orange

## **T568B**

* Pin 1 → White/Orange
* Pin 2 → Orange
* Pin 3 → White/Green
* Pin 6 → Green

👉 The **green and orange pairs are swapped** between A and B.

---

## **Important Exam Rule**

* Use the **same standard on both ends** → **Straight-through cable**
* Different standard on each end → **Crossover cable**

---

# **RJ11 Connector**

* Used for **telephone lines and DSL**
* Typically supports **2 pairs (4 wires)**
* Smaller than RJ45

5.3.7 Copper Cabling Installation Tools

## 🏢 Structured Cabling System Overview

In an office network:

1. **Computer NIC → Patch cord → Wall port**
2. **Wall port → Permanent cable (in walls/ceiling)**
3. **Permanent cable → Patch panel (equipment room)**
4. **Patch panel → Ethernet switch**

---

# 🧵 Two Types of Cable in Structured Cabling

## 1️⃣ Patch Cords

* Use **RJ45 plugs**
* Use **stranded cable** (more flexible)
* Max length per patch cord: **5 meters**
* More flexible but **more attenuation**

---

## 2️⃣ Permanent Cable

* Runs inside walls/ceilings
* Uses **solid cable** (thicker wire)
* Terminated with **IDC (punchdown blocks)**
* More reliable over distance

---

## 📏 Distance Rule (VERY IMPORTANT)

* Total link (channel) = **100 meters max**
* Includes:

  * Permanent cable
  * Both patch cords
* Patch cords max = **5m each**

---

# 🛠 Installation Tools

## 🔧 Cable Stripper

* Removes outer jacket
* Must avoid damaging inner wires
* Rotate tool gently to score jacket
* Cat 6/6A includes:

  * **Plastic star filler** (cut off with snips)
  * **Ripcord** (used to open jacket)

---

## 🔨 Punchdown Tool

* Used for **IDCs (wall ports & patch panels)**
* Steps:

  1. Untwist pairs (no more than ½ inch / 13 mm)
  2. Follow **T568A or T568B**
  3. Press wires into IDC
* Blades cut insulation and make contact

---

## 🔌 Crimper

* Used to attach **RJ45 plugs to patch cords**
* Arrange wires in correct order
* Insert into plug
* Squeeze tool to:

  * Pierce insulation
  * Lock plug onto cable

5.3.9 Network Taps

A **network tap (Test Access Point)** is a device used to **capture and monitor network traffic** on a cable.
It sends the copied traffic to a **packet analyzer or protocol analyzer** (like Wireshark).

This helps IT professionals **analyze network activity and troubleshoot problems**.

---

## Passive TAP

A **passive TAP** does not require power.

**How it works:**

* Uses an **inductor or optical splitter** to copy the signal.
* Sends the copied signal to a **monitor port**.

**Key points**

* No processing or logic decisions
* Captures **all frames**, even corrupted ones
* Performance **not affected by network load**
* **Very reliable** since it has no power requirement

---

## Active TAP

An **active TAP** is a **powered device**.

**How it works:**

* Regenerates and copies the signal before sending it to the monitor port.

**Key points**

* Used when signals are **too complex for passive taps**
* Often required for **Gigabit Ethernet over copper**
* Works with certain **fiber connections**

**Disadvantage**

* If power fails, the **tap becomes a point of failure** in the network.

---

## Mirror Port (Alternative Monitoring Method)

Network traffic can also be monitored using a **mirror port** on a switch.

**Mirror Port**

* Copies traffic from selected switch ports
* Sends the copied frames to a monitoring device
* Used for **network sniffing and analysis**

5.3.10 Copper Cabling Installation Considerations

When installing network cabling, you must **follow local building codes and fire regulations**. Specific cable types are required depending on the installation environment.

---

## **Plenum Cable**

* **Plenum space:** Area in a building for HVAC airflow (false ceilings or raised floors)
* **Why special cable is needed:** Fire can spread quickly in plenum spaces because of airflow.
* **Requirements for plenum cable:**

  * Fire-retardant
  * Self-extinguishing
  * Low smoke emission
  * Compliant with NEC fire safety standards
* **Materials:** Treated PVC or fluorinated ethylene polymer (FEP)
* **Markings:**

  * **CMP** – Plenum-rated cable
  * **CMG/CM** – General-purpose cable
* **Bandwidth:** Same as regular cable, flexibility may be slightly reduced

---

## **Direct Burial / Outside Plant (OSP) Cable**

Used for cabling **outside the building** or between buildings.

### **Challenges**

* UV exposure from sunlight
* Temperature extremes
* Moisture/damp conditions
* Rodent damage

### **Installation Types**

1. **Aerial Cable**

   * Strung between poles or anchors
   * Must be UV-resistant

2. **Conduit-protected Cable**

   * Provides protection from physical damage
   * Still vulnerable to damp and temperature extremes

3. **Direct Burial Cable**

   * Laid underground and covered with earth or concrete
   * Often gel-filled and/or armored for protection

5.3.11 Optical Cabling (Fiber Optic)

## 🔹 Why Use Fiber Instead of Copper?

Copper:

* Uses **electrical signals**
* Sensitive to **interference (EMI)**
* Signal weakens over distance (**attenuation**)

Fiber:

* Uses **light pulses (lasers or LEDs)**
* Not affected by EMI
* Much **longer distances**
* Much **higher bandwidth (Gbps–Tbps)**

---

# 🔬 Fiber Structure

A fiber optic cable contains:

* **Core** → ultra-thin glass that carries light
* **Cladding** → reflects light back into core
* **Buffer coating** → protective layer
* **Outer jacket** → overall protection

---

# 🌐 Types of Fiber

## 1️⃣ Single-Mode Fiber (SMF)

* Core size: **8–10 microns**
* Uses **laser diode**
* Wavelength: **1310 nm or 1550 nm**
* Supports:

  * **10 Gbps or higher**
  * **Long distances (kilometers)**

✔ Used for WANs and long-distance links
✔ More expensive

---

## 2️⃣ Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF)

* Core size: **50 or 62.5 microns**
* Uses **LED or VCSEL**
* Wavelength: **850 nm or 1300 nm**
* Shorter distances than SMF

✔ Cheaper
✔ Used mainly in LANs
✖ Less distance and speed than SMF

---

# 🔌 Fiber Connectors

## ST (Straight Tip)

* Push-and-twist (bayonet)
* Older multimode networks

---

## SC (Subscriber Connector)

* Push/pull design
* Simplex or duplex
* Used for single-mode or multi-mode

---

## LC (Lucent Connector)

* Smaller form factor
* Push/pull with tab
* Higher port density
* Very common in modern networks

---

# ⚠️ Important Handling Notes

* Fiber connectors are **easy to damage**
* Avoid repeated plugging/unplugging
* Always use **dust caps** on unused ports
* Can use:

  * Same connectors both ends (LC-LC)
  * Mixed connectors (SC-LC)

5.3.13 Coaxial Cabling

## 🔹 Structure of Coaxial Cable

Coaxial cable carries electrical signals using **two conductors on the same axis**:

1. **Core conductor** → Carries the signal
2. **Dielectric insulation** → Plastic layer around the core
3. **Shielding conductor** → Wire mesh, protects from EMI and serves as ground
4. **Outer jacket** → Protective sheath

> Unlike twisted pair, coax uses **shielding** instead of balancing to reduce interference.

---

## 🔹 Common Uses

* CCTV installations (security cameras)
* Cable Access TV (CATV)
* Broadband cable modems

---

## 🔹 Coaxial Connectors

### 1️⃣ F-Type Connector

* Screw-down style
* Common for cable TV and broadband
* Provides a reliable connection for high-frequency signals

### 2️⃣ BNC (Bayonet Neill-Concelman) Connector

* Bayonet coupling mechanism
* Used for **video, radio, and television cabling**
* Quick connect/disconnect, secure
